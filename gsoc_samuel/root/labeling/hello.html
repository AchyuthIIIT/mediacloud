Luke Harangody: Unfinished business brought me back to Notre Dame

SOUTH BEND, Ind. -- When he finished lunch with his coach on Sunday afternoon, just 24 hours before he'd either stay in the NBA Draft or return to Notre Dame, Luke Harangody left no firm impression as to his intentions.

In fact, there was no moment of breathtaking enlightenment until around 2 p.m. CT on Monday, mere hours before the withdrawal deadline. But once Notre Dame's star forward arrived at a choice, a great calm settled in and the business of eliminating a bad taste from his mouth became the focus.

"I'm just glad to be back here -- it feels great," Harangody said Tuesday, making his first comments after electing to return to the Irish for his senior season. "It came down to me just wanting to be a kid again. Definitely when I looked back, I didn't want to go out on the year we had last year."

The interesting part about Harangody's choice is that the lead-up to the withdrawal deadline apparently couldn't have gone much better.

In Harangody's estimation, he "definitely did more than hold my own," and the generally positive buzz about his workout performances lends credence to that.

Doubts dogged him at the start about his ability to transition to power forward in the NBA at just 6-foot-8-inches in shoes, despite two straight seasons of averaging more than 20 points and 10 rebounds a game. But Brey said teams came away impressed with Harangody's athleticism in person and his shotmaking from all over the floor.

All that said, Harangody was staring down a draft slot no better than the last third of the first round.

"He was in that area with so many moving parts, it was dangerous," Brey said.

Said Harangody: "There were a handful of teams interested in me, but nothing is guaranteed. I felt like if I kept my name in, I was taking a big chance, because I had no idea where I would be drafted."

Instead, he has a surer thing, anyway, in returning to an Irish team that can now compete in a weakened Big East and attempt to compensate for the disappointment of missing the NCAA Tournament last year. And Harangody emerges as a player who, in his own words, "couldn't be more confident" in his game.

"My footwork is a lot better, especially on the perimeter," Harangody said. "I dropped some weight and I've never been able to move this good. And just the way I'm shooting the ball -- I've never been this confident."

About Brian Hamilton
Brian Hamilton was assigned to the Notre Dame beat in July 2007 and is curious to see if fans have any interest in what happens to the football team there.

Since joining the Tribune in September 2005, Brian has covered everything from the Illinois high school cheerleading championships to the WNBA to the Final Four and Super Bowl XLI, nearly all of it without embarrassment. In the summer of 2006, he wrote a profile of a plucky, under-the-radar recruit named Jimmy Clausen, giving the kid an infusion of much-needed publicity.

Prior to arriving at the Tribune, Brian spent six years scraping permafrost off his notebook while working in Minnesota at the St. Paul Pioneer Press and mainly covering college football, basketball and the NBA's Timberwolves. This after attending one of the best schools for journalism in America, Northwestern University, and taking full advantage by majoring in English and creative writing while dropping his one journalism class after two weeks.

Brian grew up on the north side of Westfield, N.J., and now lives in Lakeview. He has many leather-bound books and his apartment smells of rich mahogany. Merlin Olsen comes over on occasion.
